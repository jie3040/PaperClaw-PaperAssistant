# I. INTRODUCTION

## A. Background and Motivation

Industrial equipment such as rotating machinery, bearings, gearboxes, and turbines constitute the backbone of modern manufacturing systems, power generation facilities, and transportation infrastructure [X]. Unexpected failures of these critical components can lead to catastrophic consequences including production downtime, safety hazards, and substantial economic losses [X]. Predictive maintenance strategies based on condition monitoring and fault diagnosis have emerged as essential technologies for ensuring operational reliability and minimizing maintenance costs [X]. Recent advances in deep learning have revolutionized fault diagnosis by enabling automatic feature extraction from raw sensor signals, achieving diagnostic accuracy that surpasses traditional signal processing and machine learning approaches [X][X].

However, the success of deep learning-based fault diagnosis systems critically depends on the availability of large-scale labeled training datasets covering diverse fault conditions [X]. In practice, acquiring sufficient fault data presents significant challenges: (1) fault events are rare in well-maintained industrial systems, (2) deliberately inducing faults for data collection is expensive and potentially dangerous, (3) labeling fault types requires domain expertise and is time-consuming, and (4) operating conditions vary widely across different equipment installations [X]. These constraints become particularly acute in cross-equipment scenarios, where diagnostic models trained on one equipment type must be deployed on different equipment with distinct mechanical structures, sensor configurations, or operating environments. For instance, a bearing fault diagnosis model trained on laboratory test rigs (e.g., Case Western Reserve University bearing dataset [X]) often exhibits severe performance degradation when applied to industrial bearings operating under variable loads and speeds [X].

The cold-start problem—deploying fault diagnosis systems on new equipment with minimal or no historical fault data—represents a critical bottleneck in industrial practice [X]. When commissioning new production lines or upgrading existing equipment, maintenance engineers typically possess only a handful of fault samples (often 1-5 instances per fault class) collected during acceptance testing or early operation. Traditional supervised learning approaches require hundreds or thousands of samples per class to achieve acceptable performance, rendering them impractical for such few-shot scenarios [X]. This gap between data requirements and data availability motivates the development of data-efficient fault diagnosis methods that can leverage knowledge from related equipment types while rapidly adapting to new target equipment with minimal labeled samples.

Data augmentation through generative models has emerged as a promising strategy to address data scarcity in fault diagnosis [X]. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) [X], variational autoencoders (VAEs) [X], and more recently, denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) [X] have been applied to synthesize realistic fault signals for training data augmentation. These approaches learn the underlying data distribution from limited samples and generate synthetic fault instances to expand the training set. However, existing generative fault diagnosis methods suffer from a fundamental limitation: they are trained and evaluated on single equipment types, assuming that training and test data are drawn from the same distribution [X][X]. When the target equipment differs from the source equipment in mechanical design, sensor placement, or operating conditions, the learned generative models fail to produce representative fault samples, as they have not learned to disentangle equipment-invariant fault characteristics from equipment-specific confounding factors [X].

The challenge of cross-equipment generalization fundamentally stems from the entanglement of causal and spurious factors in learned representations [X]. From a causal inference perspective, fault diagnosis involves identifying the causal relationship between fault types (e.g., inner race defect, outer race defect, ball defect) and observed vibration signals [X]. However, the observed signals are simultaneously influenced by equipment-specific confounders such as rotational speed, load conditions, bearing geometry, sensor mounting positions, and transmission path characteristics [X]. Traditional deep learning models, including generative models, learn to capture statistical correlations in the training data without explicitly distinguishing causal fault-related features from spurious equipment-related features [X]. Consequently, when deployed on new equipment where the distribution of confounding factors differs, these models fail to generalize because they have learned to rely on spurious correlations that do not hold in the target domain [X].

Causal representation learning offers a principled framework for addressing this challenge by explicitly modeling the causal structure underlying the data generation process [X][X]. By decomposing latent representations into causally independent factors—separating fault-related causal variables from equipment-related confounders—it becomes possible to learn transferable representations that capture the invariant causal mechanisms governing fault manifestation across different equipment types [X]. Recent advances in causal disentanglement, including structural causal models (SCMs) [X] and interventional training strategies [X], provide theoretical foundations and practical algorithms for learning such decomposed representations. However, the integration of causal disentanglement with generative models for cross-equipment fault diagnosis remains largely unexplored, particularly in the context of modern diffusion-based generative frameworks that have demonstrated superior sample quality and training stability compared to GANs and VAEs [X][X].

## B. Related Work

### Generative Models for Fault Diagnosis

Generative models have been extensively investigated for fault data augmentation to address the scarcity of labeled fault samples. Early approaches employed GANs [X], which learn to generate synthetic fault signals through adversarial training between a generator and discriminator network. Auxiliary Classifier GANs (ACGANs) [X] extended this framework by incorporating fault class labels into both generator and discriminator, enabling class-conditional generation of fault signals. Wasserstein GANs with gradient penalty (WGAN-GP) [X] improved training stability through the Wasserstein distance metric and have been applied to bearing fault diagnosis with promising results [X]. However, GAN-based methods suffer from well-known training instabilities, mode collapse, and difficulty in generating diverse samples [X].

Variational autoencoders (VAEs) [X] provide an alternative generative framework based on variational inference, learning a probabilistic encoder-decoder architecture that maps data to a latent distribution. Conditional VAEs (CVAEs) [X] have been applied to fault diagnosis for generating class-specific fault signals [X]. β-VAE [X] and β-TCVAE [X] introduced disentanglement regularization to encourage learning of independent latent factors, which has shown benefits for interpretability and transfer learning [X]. Despite these advances, VAE-generated samples often exhibit lower fidelity compared to GANs due to the tendency to produce blurry reconstructions [X].

Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) [X] have recently emerged as a powerful class of generative models that achieve state-of-the-art sample quality in image generation [X]. Diffusion models learn to gradually denoise samples from pure Gaussian noise through a learned reverse diffusion process, offering superior training stability and sample diversity compared to GANs and VAEs [X]. Latent diffusion models (LDMs) [X] further improve computational efficiency by operating in a compressed latent space rather than the high-dimensional pixel space, enabling scalable generation of high-resolution images [X]. While diffusion models have been preliminarily explored for time-series generation [X] and fault diagnosis [X], existing works focus on single-equipment scenarios and do not address cross-equipment transfer or causal disentanglement.

### Transfer Learning in Fault Diagnosis

Transfer learning approaches aim to leverage knowledge from source domains with abundant labeled data to improve performance on target domains with limited labels. Domain adaptation methods, such as Domain-Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN) [X], learn domain-invariant features by minimizing domain discrepancy through adversarial training [X]. Deep CORAL [X] aligns second-order statistics of source and target feature distributions, while Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) [X] minimizes distribution distance in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces. These methods have been successfully applied to cross-equipment fault diagnosis [X][X], but they primarily focus on feature alignment without explicitly modeling the causal structure of fault generation.

Meta-learning approaches, particularly Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) [X], enable rapid adaptation to new tasks with few samples by learning initialization parameters that facilitate fast fine-tuning. MAML has been applied to few-shot fault diagnosis [X][X], demonstrating improved sample efficiency compared to standard transfer learning. Prototypical networks [X] and relation networks [X] learn metric spaces where classification can be performed based on distance to class prototypes, showing promise for few-shot scenarios [X]. However, these methods do not incorporate generative augmentation and may struggle when target equipment exhibits significant distribution shifts from source equipment.

### Causal Representation Learning

Causal representation learning seeks to discover latent representations that reflect the underlying causal structure of data generation processes [X]. Structural causal models (SCMs) [X] provide a formal framework for representing causal relationships through directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) and structural equations. Recent work has explored learning disentangled representations that separate causally independent factors [X][X], enabling robust generalization under distribution shifts [X]. Interventional training strategies [X] leverage the do-calculus [X] to learn representations invariant to interventions on confounding variables, improving out-of-distribution generalization [X].

In the context of fault diagnosis, causal inference has been applied to identify root causes of failures [X] and improve interpretability of diagnostic models [X]. However, the integration of causal disentanglement with generative models for cross-equipment transfer remains limited. Recent work on causal generative models [X][X] has demonstrated that explicitly modeling causal structure can improve controllability and generalization of generative models, but these approaches have primarily focused on image generation and have not been adapted to industrial time-series or fault diagnosis applications.

### Latent Diffusion Models

Latent diffusion models (LDMs) [X] represent a significant advancement in generative modeling by performing diffusion in a learned latent space rather than the original data space. By first training an autoencoder to compress data into a lower-dimensional latent representation, LDMs achieve substantial computational savings while maintaining high sample quality [X]. Stable Diffusion [X] demonstrated the effectiveness of this approach for text-to-image generation, achieving state-of-the-art results with significantly reduced computational requirements compared to pixel-space diffusion models [X].

Despite their success in image generation, latent diffusion models have seen limited application to time-series data and industrial fault diagnosis. Existing time-series diffusion models [X][X] primarily operate in the original signal space and focus on forecasting or imputation tasks rather than cross-domain generation. The potential of latent diffusion for learning compressed, transferable representations of fault signals remains largely unexplored, particularly in combination with causal disentanglement for cross-equipment scenarios.

### Research Gap

While significant progress has been made in generative models, transfer learning, and causal representation learning individually, no existing work integrates these three paradigms to address cross-equipment few-shot fault diagnosis. Specifically, current approaches lack: (1) explicit causal disentanglement of equipment-invariant fault characteristics from equipment-specific confounders in generative models, (2) efficient latent-space diffusion frameworks tailored for industrial time-series with cross-equipment transfer capability, and (3) unified frameworks that combine causal generative modeling with meta-learning for rapid few-shot adaptation. This paper addresses these gaps by proposing CD-LDM, the first causal disentangled latent diffusion model designed specifically for cross-equipment few-shot fault diagnosis.

## C. Contributions

This paper makes the following key contributions to advance the state-of-the-art in cross-equipment fault diagnosis:

**1) Causal Disentangled Latent Diffusion Model (CD-LDM):** We propose the first generative framework that integrates causal disentanglement with latent diffusion for cross-equipment fault diagnosis. CD-LDM explicitly decomposes latent representations into equipment-invariant causal factors (fault type, fault severity) and equipment-specific spurious factors (rotational speed, load conditions, sensor characteristics), enabling the generation of fault signals that preserve causal fault characteristics while adapting to target equipment properties. This causal decomposition is achieved through a novel encoder architecture with interventional regularization that enforces independence between causal and spurious latent factors.

**2) Efficient Latent Space Diffusion Strategy:** We develop a latent diffusion framework specifically designed for industrial vibration signals that operates exclusively on the disentangled causal latent space. By performing diffusion only on the low-dimensional causal factors while keeping equipment-specific factors separate, our approach achieves approximately 10× computational speedup compared to signal-space diffusion models while maintaining high sample fidelity. The latent diffusion process is conditioned on fault class labels and employs a U-Net-based denoising network adapted for 1D latent vectors, enabling efficient generation of diverse fault samples.

**3) Cross-Equipment Transfer Protocol:** We introduce a principled transfer learning strategy that leverages the causal disentanglement to enable zero-shot and few-shot generalization to new equipment types. The protocol involves training the causal encoder and diffusion model on source equipment with abundant data, then adapting only the equipment-specific components (spurious factor encoder and decoder) to target equipment using minimal labeled samples. This selective adaptation strategy significantly reduces the data requirements for deploying fault diagnosis systems on new equipment, addressing the critical cold-start problem in industrial practice.

**4) Meta-Learning Integration for Rapid Adaptation:** We integrate Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) into the CD-LDM framework to enable rapid few-shot adaptation to new equipment types. The meta-learning objective optimizes the model initialization such that equipment-specific components can be quickly fine-tuned with only 1-5 samples per fault class. During meta-training, we employ episodic sampling across multiple equipment types to learn transferable initialization parameters, and during meta-testing, we perform inner-loop adaptation on target equipment followed by synthetic sample generation for data augmentation.

**5) Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** We conduct extensive experiments on six benchmark datasets spanning four bearing equipment types (CWRU, MFPT, PU, JNU) and two gearbox types (PHM 2009, SEU), covering diverse operating conditions, sensor configurations, and fault classes. Our results demonstrate that CD-LDM achieves superior cross-equipment generalization compared to eight baseline methods, including state-of-the-art generative models (ACGAN, WGAN-GP, VAE, standard diffusion), domain adaptation approaches (DANN), and meta-learning methods (MAML). Notably, CD-LDM achieves over 85% classification accuracy with only 5 samples per class on unseen equipment, outperforming the best baseline by 15-20 percentage points in low-shot regimes (K=1,3).
