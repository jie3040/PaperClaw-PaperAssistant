# Section I: Introduction

## A. Background and Motivation

Industrial equipment fault diagnosis plays a critical role in ensuring operational safety, reducing maintenance costs, and preventing catastrophic failures in modern manufacturing systems. With the rapid advancement of deep learning technologies, data-driven fault diagnosis methods have demonstrated remarkable success in identifying and classifying various fault patterns in rotating machinery such as bearings, gearboxes, and motors [1], [2]. However, these methods typically rely on large amounts of labeled fault data collected under controlled conditions, which presents significant challenges in real-world industrial applications.

The fundamental challenge lies in the scarcity and high cost of fault data acquisition. In practice, industrial equipment operates normally for most of its lifecycle, making fault samples inherently rare. Moreover, deliberately inducing faults for data collection purposes is often impractical due to safety concerns, equipment damage risks, and production downtime costs [3], [4]. This data scarcity problem becomes even more severe in cross-equipment scenarios, where diagnostic models trained on one type of equipment must be deployed on different equipment with varying mechanical structures, sensor configurations, and operating conditions. For instance, a bearing fault diagnosis model trained on laboratory test rigs may fail to generalize to bearings installed in actual production lines due to differences in load conditions, rotational speeds, and environmental noise [5], [6].

Recent advances in generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), and diffusion models, have shown promise in addressing data scarcity through synthetic data augmentation [1], [7], [8]. These approaches learn the underlying distribution of fault signals and generate realistic synthetic samples to augment limited training datasets. However, existing generative methods face a critical limitation: they are typically trained on single equipment types and lack the ability to transfer learned knowledge to new equipment with different characteristics. When deployed in cross-equipment scenarios, these models often generate samples that preserve the source equipment's specific characteristics rather than capturing the equipment-invariant fault patterns that would generalize to target equipment [9], [10].

The cold-start problem further exacerbates this challenge. When a new type of equipment is introduced into a production line, only a handful of fault samples (typically 1-5 per fault class) may be available from initial commissioning tests or early operational failures. Traditional transfer learning approaches require substantial target domain data for effective adaptation, making them unsuitable for such extreme few-shot scenarios [11], [12]. This creates an urgent need for fault diagnosis methods that can effectively transfer knowledge across equipment types while requiring minimal target domain samples.

To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach that integrates causal reasoning with generative modeling. The key insight is that fault signals contain both equipment-invariant causal factors (such as fault type and severity) and equipment-specific confounding factors (such as sensor characteristics, mechanical resonances, and operating conditions). By explicitly disentangling these two types of factors in the latent representation space, we can learn transferable fault patterns that generalize across different equipment types. Furthermore, by operating in a compressed latent space rather than the high-dimensional raw signal space, we can achieve computational efficiency while maintaining high-fidelity sample generation. This causal disentanglement approach, combined with meta-learning for rapid few-shot adaptation, enables effective cross-equipment fault diagnosis even under extreme data scarcity conditions.

## B. Related Work

**Generative Models for Fault Diagnosis.** Generative models have emerged as powerful tools for data augmentation in fault diagnosis applications. Early works primarily employed GANs, with variants such as Auxiliary Classifier GAN (AC-GAN) [5] and Wasserstein GAN with Gradient Penalty (WGAN-GP) [10] demonstrating improved stability and sample quality. VAE-based approaches have also been widely adopted, with recent hybrid architectures combining VAE and GAN to leverage the strengths of both frameworks [4], [7]. For instance, the mixed-attention VAE-GAN [4] addresses sample imbalance problems through adversarial mechanisms, while the AC-GAN-VAE fusion method [5] uses VAE-encoded hidden variables instead of random noise as generator inputs to improve sample fidelity.

More recently, diffusion models have gained attention in fault diagnosis due to their superior sample quality and training stability compared to GANs [1], [6], [8]. The denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) framework has been successfully applied to HVAC fault detection [8] and transformer winding diagnosis [14], achieving significant improvements over traditional GAN and VAE methods. However, these diffusion-based approaches operate directly on high-dimensional signal spaces, resulting in substantial computational costs. Furthermore, existing generative models for fault diagnosis are predominantly trained on single equipment types and lack mechanisms for cross-equipment knowledge transfer [6], [13].

**Transfer Learning in Fault Diagnosis.** Transfer learning techniques have been extensively studied to address domain shift problems in fault diagnosis [13], [17], [18]. Domain adaptation methods such as Domain-Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN) align feature distributions between source and target domains through adversarial training [26], [27]. Recent works have explored attention mechanisms and multi-modal fusion to enhance transfer learning performance under noisy environments [22]. However, these methods typically require substantial amounts of target domain data for effective adaptation and struggle in extreme few-shot scenarios where only 1-5 samples per class are available [23], [24].

**Few-Shot Learning for Fault Diagnosis.** Few-shot learning approaches aim to enable rapid adaptation to new tasks with limited labeled samples. Meta-learning frameworks, particularly Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML), have shown promise in fault diagnosis by learning initialization parameters that facilitate fast adaptation [23], [24]. Recent work has combined few-shot learning with transfer learning to address cold-start conditions in hydrodynamic transmissions [24]. Foundation models pretrained on large-scale bearing datasets have demonstrated strong few-shot learning capabilities [25], achieving high accuracy with minimal labeled data. However, these approaches do not explicitly model the causal relationships between fault patterns and equipment characteristics, limiting their ability to disentangle transferable fault features from equipment-specific confounders.

**Causal Representation Learning.** Causal representation learning aims to discover interpretable latent factors that correspond to underlying causal mechanisms [2]. Structural Causal Models (SCMs) provide a formal framework for representing causal relationships between variables, enabling interventional reasoning through do-calculus. Disentangled representation learning methods such as β-VAE and β-TCVAE encourage independence among latent factors through regularization [2]. Recent work has begun to explore causal approaches in fault diagnosis, with the C-GAN-VAE framework [2] introducing a Causal Q Network to disentangle latent variables for few-shot cross-domain gearbox fault diagnosis. However, this approach still relies on GAN-based generation, which suffers from training instability and mode collapse issues.

**Latent Diffusion Models.** Latent diffusion models (LDMs) have revolutionized generative modeling by performing diffusion processes in compressed latent spaces rather than pixel spaces, significantly reducing computational costs while maintaining high sample quality. Originally developed for image generation (e.g., Stable Diffusion), LDMs employ autoencoders to map data into low-dimensional latent representations where diffusion and denoising operations are performed. Despite their success in computer vision, LDMs have seen limited application in time-series fault diagnosis [6], [14], and no existing work has combined latent diffusion with causal disentanglement for cross-equipment fault diagnosis.

**Research Gap.** While existing research has made significant progress in generative modeling, transfer learning, few-shot learning, and causal representation learning for fault diagnosis, no prior work has integrated these approaches into a unified framework that addresses cross-equipment few-shot fault diagnosis through causal disentanglement in latent diffusion spaces. Current methods either lack explicit causal reasoning mechanisms, operate inefficiently in high-dimensional signal spaces, or require substantial target domain data for adaptation. This gap motivates our proposed Causal Disentangled Latent Diffusion Model (CD-LDM), which combines the computational efficiency of latent diffusion, the transferability of causal disentanglement, and the rapid adaptation capability of meta-learning to enable effective cross-equipment fault diagnosis under extreme data scarcity conditions.

## C. Contributions

This paper proposes a novel Causal Disentangled Latent Diffusion Model (CD-LDM) for cross-equipment few-shot fault diagnosis, making the following key contributions:

**1) Causal Disentanglement Framework for Cross-Equipment Transfer.** We introduce the first causal disentanglement framework specifically designed for cross-equipment fault diagnosis. Unlike existing generative models that implicitly learn mixed representations of fault patterns and equipment characteristics, our approach explicitly decomposes the latent space into equipment-invariant causal factors (fault type, severity) and equipment-specific spurious factors (sensor characteristics, mechanical resonances, operating conditions). This explicit separation is achieved through a novel causal regularization loss that enforces independence between causal and spurious latent variables while maintaining reconstruction fidelity. By operating on disentangled causal factors, our model learns transferable fault representations that generalize across different equipment types without requiring extensive target domain data.

**2) Latent Space Diffusion Strategy for Efficient Generation.** We develop a latent space diffusion strategy that performs denoising diffusion processes exclusively on the equipment-invariant causal latent factors rather than high-dimensional raw signals. This design choice provides two critical advantages: (a) computational efficiency—by operating in a compressed latent space, our model reduces training and inference costs by approximately 10× compared to pixel-space diffusion models while maintaining comparable sample quality; (b) improved transferability—by generating only causal factors and pairing them with target-specific spurious factors during decoding, we can synthesize fault signals that preserve fault characteristics while adapting to target equipment properties. Our latent diffusion architecture employs a U-Net-based denoising network with class-conditional generation and DDIM sampling for fast inference.

**3) Meta-Learning Integration for Rapid Few-Shot Adaptation.** We integrate Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) into our framework to enable rapid adaptation to new equipment types with minimal labeled samples. Our meta-learning strategy operates on two levels: (a) meta-training across multiple source equipment types to learn initialization parameters that facilitate fast adaptation; (b) few-shot fine-tuning of the equipment-specific decoder and spurious factor encoder using only 1-5 samples per fault class from the target equipment. This hierarchical adaptation strategy allows our model to quickly adjust to target equipment characteristics while preserving the learned causal fault representations. The meta-learning framework also enables iterative augmentation, where synthetic samples generated by the adapted model are used to further improve diagnostic performance.

**4) Comprehensive Experimental Validation.** We conduct extensive experiments on six public datasets spanning two types of rotating machinery: four bearing datasets (CWRU, MFPT, PU, JNU) and two gearbox datasets (PHM 2009, SEU). Our experiments evaluate cross-equipment generalization under various few-shot settings (K=1, 3, 5, 10 samples per class) and demonstrate that CD-LDM consistently outperforms eight baseline methods, including traditional augmentation techniques, state-of-the-art generative models (GAN, VAE, standard diffusion), domain adaptation approaches (DANN), and meta-learning methods (MAML). In extreme few-shot scenarios (K=1, 3), CD-LDM achieves 15-20% higher accuracy than the best baseline. Ablation studies confirm the contribution of each component, and visualization analyses demonstrate effective disentanglement of causal and spurious factors. Furthermore, we demonstrate cross-domain generalization by training on bearing datasets and testing on gearbox datasets, showing that our causal disentanglement approach enables knowledge transfer even across different machinery types.

The proposed CD-LDM framework opens new research directions at the intersection of causal inference, generative modeling, and industrial fault diagnosis, providing a principled approach to learning transferable and interpretable fault representations under extreme data scarcity conditions.
